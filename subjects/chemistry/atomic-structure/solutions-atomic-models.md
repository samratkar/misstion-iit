# Solution Key: Atomic Structure (Atomic Models)

## Phase 1: Foundation

### Group 1.1: The Discovery of Subatomic Particles

*   **Q1.1.1 Solution:** Dalton's model could not explain this. The production of rays implies that atoms contain constituent charged particles (electrons and positive ions) that can be stripped away or mobilized under the influence of high voltage in a vacuum.
*   **Q1.1.2 Solution:** Thomson balanced the electric force ($F_e = eE$) and magnetic force ($F_m = evB$). For a straight path, $eE = evB \implies v = \frac{E}{B}$. By measuring the radius of curvature $r$ in a magnetic field alone ($r = \frac{mv}{eB}$), he substituted $v$ to get:
    $$\frac{e}{m} = \frac{E}{B^2 r}$$
*   **Q1.1.3 Solution:** Quantization means that electric charge is not continuous but exists in discrete packets. It proves that there is a fundamental, indivisible unit of charge (the elementary charge, $e \approx 1.6 \times 10^{-19}$ C).
*   **Q1.1.4 Solution:** When electrons are removed, positively charged ions (cations) remain. These ions are accelerated toward the cathode; if the cathode has holes (canals), they pass through, forming "canal rays."
*   **Q1.1.5 Solution:** Cathode rays consist of electrons, which are identical for all matter. Anode rays consist of positive ions, whose mass and charge depend on the specific gas atoms from which they were formed.

### Group 1.2: Early Atomic Models & The Nucleus

*   **Q1.2.1 Solution:** Thomson envisioned a uniform sphere of positive charge (the "pudding") with individual electrons (the "plums") embedded throughout to maintain overall neutrality.
*   **Q1.2.2 Solution:** No. If the positive charge were spread over the entire volume of the atom, the electric field at any point would be too weak to exert the massive force required to deflect a heavy, fast-moving alpha particle backwards.
*   **Q1.2.3 Solution:** He was referring to the discovery that a small fraction (about 1 in 20,000) of alpha particles were deflected by more than $90^\circ$, with some bouncing almost directly back toward the source.
*   **Q1.2.4 Solution:** The nucleus must occupy an infinitesimal fraction of the atom's volume. While the atom is $\sim 10^{-10}$ m in diameter, the nucleus is only $\sim 10^{-15}$ m.
*   **Q1.2.5 Solution:** The ratio of the atom's radius to the nucleus's radius is $10^5$. If the nucleus is a cricket ball (radius $\approx 3.6$ cm), the atom would have a radius of $3.6 \times 10^5$ cm, which is $3.6$ km.

### Group 1.3: The Classical Stability Crisis

*   **Q1.3.1 Solution:** In circular motion, the direction of the velocity vector is constantly changing. A change in velocity over time is defined as acceleration ($a = \frac{v^2}{r}$), even if speed is constant.
*   **Q1.3.2 Solution:** The electron would continuously lose energy via electromagnetic radiation. This loss of kinetic energy would cause it to spiral inward, eventually collapsing into the nucleus in approximately $10^{-11}$ seconds.
*   **Q1.3.3 Solution:** A spiral would produce a continuous range of frequencies as the orbital period changed. The observation of discrete, sharp spectral lines proved that electrons only exist in specific, quantized energy states.
*   **Q1.3.4 Solution:** The Neutron.
*   **Q1.3.5 Solution:**
    *   **Atomic Number ($Z$):** Number of protons = $11$ (Sodium).
    *   **Mass Number ($A$):** Protons + Neutrons = $11 + 12 = 23$.
    *   **Net Charge:** Protons - Electrons = $11 - 10 = +1$.

## Phase 2: Concept Building

### Group 2.1: The Quantum Nature of Light

*   **Q2.1.1 Solution:** Color is our brain's interpretation of the frequency ($\nu$) or wavelength ($\lambda$) of light. They are related by $c = \nu\lambda$. High frequency (short $\lambda$) is perceived as violet; low frequency (long $\lambda$) as red.
*   **Q2.1.2 Solution:** A "Photon." The energy is given by $E = h\nu$.
*   **Q2.1.3 Solution:** In the particle model, one photon interacts with one electron. If an individual photon's energy ($h\nu$) is less than the work function ($W_0$), no amount of "brightness" (more low-energy photons) can dislodge an electron.
*   **Q2.1.4 Solution:** The Work Function represents the minimum energy binding an electron to the bulk of the metal.
*   **Q2.1.5 Solution:** 
    *   Energy of photon $E = \frac{hc}{\lambda} = \frac{(6.626 \times 10^{-34} \text{ J s})(3 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s})}{500 \times 10^{-9} \text{ m}} = 3.975 \times 10^{-19}$ J.
    *   Convert to eV: $\frac{3.975 \times 10^{-19}}{1.602 \times 10^{-19}} \approx 2.48$ eV.
    *   Since $E (2.48 \text{ eV}) > W_0 (2.13 \text{ eV})$, electrons **will** be ejected.
*   **Q2.1.6 Solution:** Stopping potential ($V_0$) is the negative voltage required to stop the most energetic photoelectrons. It is related to maximum kinetic energy by $K.E_{max} = eV_0$.

### Group 2.2: The Bohr Model for Hydrogen-like Species

*   **Q2.2.1 Solution:** The angular momentum ($L$) must be an integral multiple of $\frac{h}{2\pi}$:
    $$L = mvr = \frac{nh}{2\pi}, \quad n = 1, 2, 3, \dots$$
*   **Q2.2.2 Solution:**
    1. Force balance: $\frac{mv^2}{r} = \frac{kZe^2}{r^2} \implies v^2 = \frac{kZe^2}{mr}$.
    2. From quantization: $v = \frac{nh}{2\pi mr} \implies v^2 = \frac{n^2 h^2}{4\pi^2 m^2 r^2}$.
    3. Equating: $\frac{kZe^2}{mr} = \frac{n^2 h^2}{4\pi^2 m^2 r^2} \implies r_n = \frac{n^2 h^2}{4\pi^2 m k Z e^2}$.
*   **Q2.2.3 Solution:** $r_n = a_0 \frac{n^2}{Z}$. For $Li^{2+}$ ($Z=3$) and $n=2$:
    $$r = a_0 \frac{2^2}{3} = \frac{4}{3}a_0 \approx 1.333 a_0$$
*   **Q2.2.4 Solution:** $v_n = \frac{2\pi k Z e^2}{nh}$. Since $v \propto \frac{1}{n}$, the speed **decreases** as the electron moves to higher energy levels (larger $n$).
*   **Q2.2.5 Solution:** 
    *   $K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 = \frac{kZe^2}{2r}$.
    *   $U = -\frac{kZe^2}{r}$.
    *   $E = K + U = \frac{kZe^2}{2r} - \frac{kZe^2}{r} = -\frac{kZe^2}{2r}$.
    *   Therefore, $E = -K = \frac{U}{2}$.
*   **Q2.2.6 Solution:** The negative sign indicates a "bound state"; work must be done to pull the electron away. $E=0$ corresponds to the "ionization limit," where the electron is free from the nucleus and at rest at infinity.
*   **Q2.2.7 Solution:** Bohr's model only considers the nucleus-electron attraction. It fails in multi-electron atoms because it cannot account for electron-electron repulsion and the complex shielding effects.

### Group 2.3: Atomic Spectra & Transitions

*   **Q2.3.1 Solution:** 
    $$\bar{\nu} = \frac{1}{\lambda} = R_H Z^2 \left( \frac{1}{n_1^2} - \frac{1}{n_2^2} \right)$$
*   **Q2.3.2 Solution:**
    *   **Lyman Series:** $n_1 = 1$; Ultraviolet (UV) region.
    *   **Balmer Series:** $n_1 = 2$; Visible region (first few lines).
*   **Q2.3.3 Solution:** For Hydrogen ($Z=1$), Balmer series ($n_1=2$), limiting line ($n_2 = \infty$):
    $$\frac{1}{\lambda} = R_H (1) \left( \frac{1}{2^2} - \frac{1}{\infty^2} \right) = \frac{R_H}{4}$$
    $$\lambda = \frac{4}{R_H} = \frac{4}{1.097 \times 10^7 \text{ m}^{-1}} \approx 364.6 \text{ nm}$$
*   **Q2.3.4 Solution:** $\Delta E$ between successive levels **decreases** as $n$ increases ($E_n \propto -1/n^2$). This means spectral lines become increasingly crowded (converge) at higher frequencies.
*   **Q2.3.5 Solution:** Total lines $= \frac{n(n-1)}{2}$. For $n=4$:
    $$\frac{4(4-1)}{2} = 6 \text{ lines}$$
*   **Q2.3.6 Solution:** The effective Rydberg constant for a species scales with $Z^2$. For $He^+$ ($Z=2$):
    $$R_{He^+} = R_H \times 2^2 = 4 R_H$$
*   **Q2.3.7 Solution:** Because the energy required to excite an electron from level $A$ to $B$ (absorption) is exactly equal to the energy released when it falls from $B$ to $A$ (emission).

## Phase 3: Advanced Application

### Group 3.1: Wave Mechanics & Uncertainty

*   **Q3.1.1 Solution:**
    $$\lambda = \frac{h}{p} = \frac{h}{mv}$$
*   **Q3.1.2 Solution:** $\lambda = \frac{h}{\sqrt{2mK}}$. For the same Kinetic Energy ($K$), $\lambda \propto \frac{1}{\sqrt{m}}$. Since the electron is much lighter than the proton, the **electron** has a significantly longer wavelength.
*   **Q3.1.3 Solution:** Bohr's stable orbits are those where the electron wave forms a standing wave. This requires the circumference to be an integer multiple of the wavelength:
    $$2\pi r = n\lambda \implies 2\pi r = n\left(\frac{h}{mv}\right) \implies mvr = \frac{nh}{2\pi}$$
    This matches Bohr's quantization condition.
*   **Q3.1.4 Solution:** $\Delta x \cdot \Delta p \geq \frac{h}{4\pi}$. If we attempt to define a precise "path" (exact position and momentum at every instant), the uncertainty in the other variable becomes infinite, making the "path" physically undefined.
*   **Q3.1.5 Solution:**
    $$\Delta v \geq \frac{h}{4\pi m \Delta x} = \frac{6.626 \times 10^{-34}}{4\pi (9.11 \times 10^{-31})(10^{-11} \text{ m})} \approx 5.8 \times 10^6 \text{ m/s}$$
    This uncertainty is on the same order of magnitude as the velocity itself, making the concept of a fixed circular orbit meaningless.

### Group 3.2: Quantum Mechanical Model & Orbitals

*   **Q3.2.1 Solution:** $|\psi|^2$ represents the **probability density**—the probability of finding the electron within a small volume element around a given point in space.
*   **Q3.2.2 Solution:** An "Orbit" is a fixed 2D circular path. An "Orbital" is a 3D region of space where there is a high probability (usually $\sim 90\%$) of finding the electron.
*   **Q3.2.3 Solution:**
    1.  **$n$ (Principal):** Size and energy level.
    2.  **$l$ (Azimuthal):** Shape of the orbital (subshell).
    3.  **$m_l$ (Magnetic):** Orientation of the orbital in space.
    4.  **$m_s$ (Spin):** Direction of electron spin ($+1/2$ or $-1/2$).
*   **Q3.2.4 Solution:** For $n=3$:
    *   $l=0 \implies 3s$ subshell.
    *   $l=1 \implies 3p$ subshell.
    *   $l=2 \implies 3d$ subshell.
*   **Q3.2.5 Solution:** A node is a region where the probability of finding an electron is zero ($|\psi|^2 = 0$).
    *   **Radial Nodes:** $n - l - 1$.
    *   **Angular Nodes:** $l$.
    *   **For $3p$ orbital:** $n=3, l=1$. Radial nodes $= 3-1-1 = 1$; Angular nodes $= 1$. Total nodes $= 2$.

### Group 3.3: Rules for Electronic Configuration

*   **Q3.3.1 Solution:** Aufbau Principle states orbitals are filled in order of increasing energy. We use the $(n+l)$ rule:
    *   $4s: 4+0 = 4$.
    *   $3d: 3+2 = 5$.
    Since $4 < 5$, the **$4s$ orbital is filled first.**
*   **Q3.3.2 Solution:** A single orbital can hold a maximum of **2 electrons**, and they must have **opposite (antiparallel) spins**.
*   **Q3.3.3 Solution:** Electrons fill degenerate (equal energy) orbitals singly with parallel spins before pairing. This minimizes electron-electron repulsion and maximizes stability through exchange energy.
*   **Q3.3.4 Solution:**
    *   **Cr ($Z=24$):** $[Ar] 3d^5 4s^1$ (instead of $3d^4 4s^2$).
    *   **Cu ($Z=29$):** $[Ar] 3d^{10} 4s^1$ (instead of $3d^9 4s^2$).
    *   **Reason:** Half-filled ($d^5$) and fully-filled ($d^{10}$) subshells have extra stability due to their symmetrical distribution and high exchange energy.
*   **Q3.3.5 Solution:**
    *   **Oxygen ($1s^2 2s^2 2p^4$):** According to Hund's rule, the $2p$ subshell has two unpaired electrons. Unpaired electrons cause **paramagnetism**.
    *   **Neon ($1s^2 2s^2 2p^6$):** All orbitals are completely filled; all electrons are paired. This results in **diamagnetism**.
