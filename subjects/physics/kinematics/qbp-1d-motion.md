# Question-Based Pedagogy: Motion in a Straight Line

## Objective
Master the kinematics of one-dimensional motion, from fundamental definitions of position and velocity to advanced calculus-based applications and relative motion, using a guided inquiry approach.

---

# Phase 1: Foundation (15 Questions)
*Focus: Distance vs Displacement, Average speed/velocity, Instantaneous velocity.*

### Group 1.1: The Geometry of Motion
- **Q1.1:** A particle moves from $x = -2\text{m}$ to $x = +5\text{m}$. What is its displacement? 
- **Q1.2:** If the particle in Q1.1 then returns to $x = 0$, what is the total distance traveled and the net displacement from the start?
- **Q1.3:** An athlete runs around a circular track of radius $R$. What are the distance and displacement after completing exactly $1.5$ revolutions?
- **Q1.4:** Under what specific condition is the magnitude of displacement exactly equal to the distance traveled?
- **Q1.5:** Can the magnitude of displacement ever exceed the distance traveled? Explain why or why not using the concept of path length.

**Concept Takeaway:** Distance is a scalar representing the total path length, while displacement is a vector representing the change in position ($\Delta x = x_f - x_i$). For any motion, $\text{Distance} \ge |\text{Displacement}|$.

### Group 1.2: Rate of Change - Average Values
- **Q1.6:** A car travels the first half of a journey at speed $v_1$ and the second half at speed $v_2$. Is the average speed the arithmetic mean $\frac{v_1 + v_2}{2}$? 
- **Q1.7:** Derive the formula for average speed for the car in Q1.6 in terms of $v_1$ and $v_2$. 
- **Q1.8:** Now consider a car that travels for half the total *time* at $v_1$ and the remaining half *time* at $v_2$. How does this average speed differ from the result in Q1.7?
- **Q1.9:** A particle moves from $A$ to $B$ at $40\text{ km/h}$ and returns from $B$ to $A$ at $60\text{ km/h}$. What is its average velocity for the entire round trip?
- **Q1.10:** Why is it possible for a particle to have a non-zero average speed but zero average velocity over a time interval?

**Concept Takeaway:** Average speed is $\frac{\text{Total Distance}}{\text{Total Time}}$, whereas average velocity is $\frac{\text{Net Displacement}}{\text{Total Time}}$. The harmonic mean applies to equal distance segments, while the arithmetic mean applies to equal time segments.

### Group 1.3: Transition to Instantaneous Motion
- **Q1.11:** As the time interval $\Delta t$ approaches zero, what does the ratio $\frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t}$ become?
- **Q1.12:** If a car's speedometer reads a constant $60\text{ km/h}$, does this represent average or instantaneous speed?
- **Q1.13:** The position of a particle is given by $x(t) = 4t^2 + 2t$. Find the velocity at $t = 2\text{s}$.
- **Q1.14:** If the velocity of a particle is constant, how does its instantaneous velocity at any moment compare to its average velocity over any interval?
- **Q1.15:** Define acceleration as the "rate of change of a rate of change." What is its mathematical expression in terms of velocity $v$ and time $t$?

**Concept Takeaway:** Instantaneous velocity $v = \frac{dx}{dt}$ is the slope of the position-time graph at a specific moment. Acceleration $a = \frac{dv}{dt}$ measures how quickly velocity changes.

# Phase 2: Concept Building (20 Questions)
*Focus: Equations of motion, Motion under gravity, Graph analysis.*

### Group 2.1: The Kinematic Equations (Constant Acceleration)
- **Q2.1:** For constant acceleration $a$, integrate $dv = a \, dt$ from $t=0$ (velocity $u$) to $t=T$ (velocity $v$) to find the first equation of motion.
- **Q2.2:** Using the relation $v = \frac{dx}{dt}$, substitute $v = u + at$ and integrate to derive $s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2$.
- **Q2.3:** Prove that $v^2 = u^2 + 2as$ by using the identity $a = v \frac{dv}{dx}$.
- **Q2.4:** A car traveling at $20\text{ m/s}$ applies brakes and stops in $5\text{s}$. Calculate the uniform acceleration and the distance traveled during braking.
- **Q2.5:** Derive an expression for the displacement in the $n^{th}$ second ($s_n = u + \frac{a}{2}(2n - 1)$).

**Concept Takeaway:** The three equations of motion ($v=u+at$, $s=ut+\frac{1}{2}at^2$, $v^2=u^2+2as$) are only valid when acceleration is **constant**.

### Group 2.2: Mastery of Constant Acceleration
- **Q2.6:** A particle starts from rest and moves with constant acceleration. What is the ratio of distances covered in the 1st, 2nd, and 3rd seconds?
- **Q2.7:** A bullet loses $1/20^{th}$ of its velocity after passing through a plank. What is the minimum number of such planks required to stop the bullet? (Assume constant resistance).
- **Q2.8:** A train passes a telegraph post with velocity $u$ and the last compartment passes it with velocity $v$. What is the velocity of the middle point of the train as it passes the post?
- **Q2.9:** If a body covers equal distances in equal intervals of time, what can you conclude about its acceleration?
- **Q2.10:** If a body covers equal *extra* distances in each successive second (e.g., $5\text{m}, 7\text{m}, 9\text{m}$), what can you conclude about its acceleration?

**Concept Takeaway:** For motion starting from rest with constant $a$, distances in successive seconds follow the ratio of odd numbers ($1:3:5...$), known as Galileo's Law of Odd Numbers.

### Group 2.3: Motion Under Gravity
- **Q2.11:** A ball is thrown vertically upwards with velocity $u$. At the peak of its flight, what are its velocity and acceleration?
- **Q2.12:** Calculate the time taken to reach the maximum height $H$ and the value of $H$ in terms of $u$ and $g$.
- **Q2.13:** Show that the time of ascent is exactly equal to the time of descent for a body returning to its launch point (neglecting air resistance).
- **Q2.14:** A stone is dropped from a height $h$. Another is thrown down with initial velocity $u$. How does the acceleration of the two stones compare during their fall?
- **Q2.15:** A tap drips at a steady rate. As the drops fall, does the distance between two consecutive drops increase, decrease, or remain constant? Why?

**Concept Takeaway:** Motion under gravity is a case of constant acceleration ($a = -g$). The symmetry of time and speed at any height is a hallmark of this motion when air resistance is neglected.

### Group 2.4: The Language of Graphs
- **Q2.16:** What does a horizontal line on a $v-t$ graph imply about the particle's acceleration?
- **Q2.17:** How do you determine the total distance traveled from a $v-t$ graph if the velocity becomes negative?
- **Q2.18:** If the $x-t$ graph is a parabola opening upwards, what is the sign of the acceleration?
- **Q2.19:** What physical quantity is represented by the slope of the $v-t$ graph?
- **Q2.20:** Sketch the $a-t$ graph for a ball thrown upwards that bounces off the ground elastically.

**Concept Takeaway:** Slope of $x-t \to v$; Slope of $v-t \to a$. Area under $v-t \to \Delta x$ (displacement); Area under $|v|-t \to \text{distance}$. Area under $a-t \to \Delta v$.

# Phase 3: Advanced Application (15 Questions)
*Focus: Non-uniform acceleration (calculus), Relative motion in 1D, stopping distance, and reaction time.*

### Group 3.1: Calculus-Based Kinematics
- **Q3.1:** A particle's acceleration is given by $a = 3t^2 + 2t$. If it starts from rest, find its velocity as a function of time.
- **Q3.2:** If velocity is given by $v(x) = k \sqrt{x}$, where $k$ is a constant, find the acceleration of the particle.
- **Q3.3:** For a particle moving with $a = -kv^2$, find the velocity as a function of displacement $x$ given $v(0) = v_0$.
- **Q3.4:** The position of a particle is $x = A \sin(\omega t)$. Derive the expressions for velocity and acceleration. At what position is the acceleration maximum?
- **Q3.5:** A particle moves such that its displacement $x$ varies with time $t$ as $t = \sqrt{x} + 3$. Find the velocity when $x = 0$.

**Concept Takeaway:** When acceleration depends on time, position, or velocity, we must use integration ($v = \int a \, dt$, $x = \int v \, dt$) or the differential form $a = v \frac{dv}{dx}$.

### Group 3.2: Relative Motion in 1D
- **Q3.6:** If Car A moves at $v_A$ and Car B moves at $v_B$ in the same direction, what is the velocity of A relative to B ($v_{AB}$)?
- **Q3.7:** Two trains, each $100\text{m}$ long, moving in opposite directions at $10\text{ m/s}$ and $15\text{ m/s}$ cross each other. What is their relative speed, and how long does the crossing take?
- **Q3.8:** A ball is dropped from a height $H$ while another is thrown up from the ground with $u$. What is the relative acceleration of the two balls?
- **Q3.9:** Using the concept of relative velocity, find the time they meet in Q3.8.
- **Q3.10:** A bird flies at $20\text{ km/h}$ between two trains approaching each other at $10\text{ km/h}$ each from a distance of $40\text{ km}$. What is the total distance the bird flies before the trains collide?

**Concept Takeaway:** $v_{AB} = v_A - v_B$. In 1D, relative acceleration between two objects in free fall is zero, meaning they move at a constant relative velocity.

### Group 3.3: Stopping Distance and Reaction Time
- **Q3.11:** Define "Reaction Time" and explain why it adds to the total distance traveled before a car comes to a stop.
- **Q3.12:** If the reaction time is $t_r$ and the initial speed is $u$, how much distance is covered *before* the brakes are even applied?
- **Q3.13:** Derive the expression for "Stopping Distance" $d_s$ in terms of initial speed $u$, deceleration $a$, and reaction time $t_r$.
- **Q3.14:** If a car's speed is doubled, by what factor does the braking distance (excluding reaction time) increase?
- **Q3.15:** A driver traveling at $30\text{ m/s}$ sees an obstacle $100\text{m}$ away. If his reaction time is $0.2\text{s}$ and the maximum deceleration is $5\text{ m/s}^2$, can he stop in time?

**Concept Takeaway:** Total stopping distance = (Distance during reaction time) + (Braking distance). $d_{total} = u t_r + \frac{u^2}{2a}$. Braking distance is proportional to the square of the initial speed ($u^2$).
